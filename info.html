Life of Works of
     Dr. Jose Rizal

"Rizal Mastery Challenge: Answer"

"Noli Me Tangere"

Noli Me Tangere" is a novel written by the Filipino national hero, Dr. José Rizal. Published in 1887, it is considered one of the most influential works of Philippine literature. The title, which translates to "Touch Me Not" in English, is derived from a Latin phrase that appears in the Bible, specifically when Jesus Christ tells Mary Magdalene not to touch him after his resurrection.

"Berlin"

"Noli Me Tangere" was published in Berlin, Germany. José Rizal, the author of the novel, completed the manuscript while he was studying and living in Europe. Due to the strict censorship laws in the Philippines under Spanish colonial rule, Rizal opted to have the novel published abroad to avoid potential suppression or backlash from the colonial authorities. In 1887, the novel was printed and published by the printing press of Berliner Buchdruckerei-Actiengesellschaft in Berlin. Its publication in Berlin allowed the novel to reach a wider audience and played a significant role in shaping Filipino national consciousness and sparking resistance against Spanish oppression.

"Laong Laan"

The pen name used by José Rizal in his writings for La Solidaridad, a reformist newspaper published in Spain, was "Laong Laan." Rizal contributed numerous articles and essays to the newspaper, addressing various social, political, and economic issues in the Philippines during the late 19th century. "Laong Laan" translates to "Ever Prepared" or "Always Ready" in English, reflecting Rizal's steadfast commitment to advocating for reforms and promoting national consciousness among the Filipino people.

"Sa Aking mga Kabata"

The first poem written by José Rizal, often considered his earliest work, is titled "Sa Aking Mga Kabata" ("To My Fellow Youth" or "To My Childhood Companions"). Rizal wrote this poem in his native language, Tagalog, when he was just eight years old.
The poem encourages young Filipinos to embrace their mother tongue, preserve their cultural heritage, and strive for knowledge. It emphasizes the importance of education in shaping the future of the nation. "Sa Aking Mga Kabata" serves as a precursor to Rizal's later works, which would delve deeper into the issues of national identity, social reform, and the fight against colonial oppression.

"Eleven"

The Rizal family had a total of eleven siblings, making José Rizal the seventh child out of the eleven. Saturnina Rizal Paciano Rizal Narcisa Rizal Olympia Rizal Lucia Rizal María Rizal José Rizal Concepción Rizal Joséfa Rizal Trinidad Rizal Soledad Rizal José Rizal's siblings played a significant role in his life, and some of them also played active roles in the Philippine Revolution and the fight for independence from Spanish colonial rule.

"June 19, 1861"

Rizal's birthday is on June 19, 1861 in Calamba, Laguna, Philippines. He is widely regarded as a symbol of Philippine nationalism and a prominent figure in the country's fight for independence from Spanish colonization.

"Crisostomo Ibarra"

The main character of "Noli Me Tangere" is Crisostomo Ibarra. Crisostomo is a young Filipino who returns to his homeland after studying in Europe. He is the heir of a wealthy and influential family in the town of San Diego, but his experiences abroad have shaped his worldview and instilled in him a desire for social reform and justice.

"35"

Jose Rizal died at the age of 35. He was born on June 19, 1861, and was executed on December 30, 1896. Rizal's life was tragically cut short due to his involvement in the Philippine Revolution against Spanish colonial rule. Despite his relatively young age, Rizal left an indelible mark on Philippine history and became an enduring symbol of Filipino nationalism and the fight for independence.

"Uzman"

Rizal's dog's name was "Usman." Usman was a Japanese Spitz, and Rizal had a fondness for him. Rizal's love for animals, including his pet dog, is known from his letters and writings where he would mention Usman affectionately. Usman became a loyal companion to Rizal during his exile in Dapitan, Philippines.

"Valentin Ventura"

The "heaven-sent financer" of the publication of "Noli Me Tangere" was Valentin Ventura. Valentin Ventura was a wealthy Filipino businessman and supporter of José Rizal. He provided financial assistance to Rizal to help cover the expenses of printing and publishing the novel. His generosity and support were crucial in making the publication of "Noli Me Tangere" possible, as Rizal faced financial constraints and difficulty finding sponsors due to the controversial nature of the novel's content. Valentin Ventura's contribution played a significant role in ensuring the dissemination of the novel and its impact on Filipino society.

"Ang huling Paalam"

Dr. Jose Rizal, being a prolific writer, composed numerous poems throughout his life. One of the most widely celebrated and acclaimed poems penned by Rizal is "Mi Último Adiós" or "My Last Farewell." It is considered his farewell poem, written on the eve of his execution, and reflects his profound love for his country and his dedication to its freedom. In "Mi Último Adiós," Rizal bids farewell to his beloved Philippines, expressing his sentiments of sacrifice, martyrdom, and hope for a better future. The poem encapsulates his unwavering patriotism and serves as a testament to his commitment to the Filipino people. It resonates with profound emotion and displays Rizal's mastery of language and poetic expression.

"Fr. Rufino Collantes"

The priest who baptized José Rizal was Father Rufino Collantes. Rizal was born on June 19, 1861, in Calamba, Laguna, Philippines. His parents, Francisco Mercado and Teodora Alonso, arranged for his baptism at the local church shortly after his birth. Father Rufino Collantes, a Catholic priest, performed the baptism ceremony and officially named the newborn child José Protasio Rizal Mercado y Alonso Realonda. This event marked the beginning of Rizal's journey in life, leading him to become one of the most significant figures in Philippine history..

"Leonor Rivera"

The person who is often considered as the inspiration for the character of Maria Clara in José Rizal's "Noli Me Tangere" is Leonor Rivera. Leonor Rivera was Rizal's childhood sweetheart and love interest. She was known for her beauty, intelligence, and talents. Rizal and Rivera had a deep and affectionate relationship, which endured despite the challenges and obstacles they faced, including Rizal's travels and eventual exile. In "Noli Me Tangere," the character of Maria Clara represents the idealized Filipina of that era – gentle, virtuous, and embodying the values of purity and self-sacrifice. Rizal used Maria Clara as a symbol of the plight of Filipino women and the societal constraints they faced under Spanish colonial rule.

"Paciano"

The character of Pilosopo Tasyo in "Noli Me Tangere" is inspired by Rizal's older brother, Paciano Rizal. Paciano Rizal, being José Rizal's eldest brother, played a significant role in shaping Rizal's nationalist and reformist views. He served as a source of inspiration and mentor to Rizal, influencing his perspective on social issues and the need for change in Philippine society.

"November 15, 1895"

The last issue of La Solidaridad, the newspaper of the Propaganda Movement, was published on November 15, 1895. La Solidaridad was a significant publication during the late 19th century and served as a platform for Filipino reformists to voice their concerns about social, political, and economic issues in the Philippines. It played a crucial role in advocating for reforms and raising awareness among Filipinos and the international community about the injustices of Spanish colonial rule. The publication ceased due to financial difficulties and government suppression, but its impact on the national consciousness and the pursuit of Philippine independence remained significant.

"Binan"

Jose Rizal began his formal schooling at the age of three. In 1865, he enrolled in a private school called "Casa Escuela" in his hometown of Calamba, Laguna, Philippines. Casa Escuela was run by his mother, Teodora Alonso, who was a highly educated woman. Under her guidance, Rizal received his early education, learning the basics of reading, writing, and arithmetic. This early foundation would later pave the way for his pursuit of higher education and his remarkable intellectual achievements.

"Concepcion"

The first sorrow of Jose Rizal, as mentioned in his autobiography "A Memoir of My Childhood," was the death of his younger sister, Conception. Conception Mercado Rizal was born in 1862 and was the second child of Francisco Mercado and Teodora Alonso. However, she died at a very young age, just a few hours after birth. Rizal described her death as his "first conscious sorrow," marking an early experience of loss and grief that would shape his sensitivity and empathy towards others throughout his life.

"His Mother"

Jose Rizal's first teacher was his mother, Teodora Alonso. Teodora Alonso was a highly educated woman who recognized the importance of education and took it upon herself to teach her children at home. She taught Rizal and his siblings basic reading, writing, and arithmetic skills during their early years. Teodora Alonso's influence as Rizal's first teacher played a significant role in nurturing his love for learning and laying the foundation for his future education and intellectual pursuits.

"Five"

Jose Rizal was able to read and write at a very young age. Rizal showed exceptional intellectual aptitude and a strong passion for learning from a very early age he learned the alphabet from his mother at 3. It is said that he could already read and write by the age of five. This early proficiency in literacy was nurtured by his mother, Teodora Alonso, who played a crucial role in his early education. Teodora recognized Rizal's intellectual potential and provided him with the necessary guidance and support to develop his reading and writing skills. Rizal's early mastery of literacy laid the foundation for his later achievements as a writer, poet, and national hero of the Philippines.

"Francisco"

Rizal and Josephine lived as husband and wife in a common-law marriage in Talisay in Dapitan. The couple had a son, but he lived only a few hours. Rizal named him after his father Francisco.
